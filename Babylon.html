Babylonian Rectangle Method

The Babylonians had an interesting algorithm to find the length and the width of a rectangle if you knew the sum of the length and width and the area of the rectangle:

  1. Take half of the sum of your length and width.
  2. Square this result.
  3. Subtract the area of the rectangle from this.
  4. Take the square root.
  5. Subtract this from the half-sum of the length and width to get one side, and add it to the half-sum of the length and width to get the other side.

So how exactly does the algorithm work? If we let the width and length be w and l respectively and simplify our algorithm, we can figure it out.

We know two things: w+l and wl (the area is width times length, so this is equivalent to the expression wl.)

  1. The first instruction says "take half the sum." That's: (w+l)/2
  2. The second says:"square this result". That simplifies to: (w^2+2wl+l^2)/4
  3. Then the third says: "subtract the area of the rectangle from this: (w^2+2wl+l^2)/4-wl = (w^2+2wl+l^2)/4-(4wl)/4 = (w^2-2wl+l^2)/4
  4. Now it tells us to take the square root: The square root of the denominator is 2. The square root of the numerator, we might recognize, is (w-l). So now we've got: (w-l)/2
  5. Now it says we can subtract this expression from the half-sum of l,w to get one side and add it to the half-sum of l,w to get the other.
  6. These two simplify to: (w-l)/2-(w+l)/2=2l/2=l. The length! And (w-l)/2+(w+l)/2=2w/2=w. The width!

So the algorithm is just a set of clever manipulations, (you might have caught the difference of squares!), in the form of a well crafted recipe. The Babylonians couldn't have known this easily, of course. Assigning variables to values was a virtually unheard of concept at the time, as Algebra would take a while to make its grand entry into the world of mathematics.
